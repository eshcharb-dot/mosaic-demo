# Mosaic — CEO Investor Relations Playbook
*Cycles 133–135: VC relationship framework, first meeting strategy, process management, 20 target investors*

---

## The Core Principle

Fundraising is a relationship business that takes 12–18 months to produce results. The CEOs who raise the best rounds at the best terms don't start building VC relationships when they need money — they start before they have a product. The goal of this playbook is to be building genuine relationships with 20 target investors for 12 months before you ever say the word "raising."

---

## Part 1: The Relationship-First Framework

### Why 12 Months Before You Need Money

A VC who has watched you execute for 12 months and hit every milestone you said you'd hit trusts you. A VC who meets you for the first time on the day you say "I'm raising" is betting on a stranger.

The math: a 12-month relationship where you send monthly updates and have 2–3 substantive conversations converts at 40–60% (they already believe in you). A cold pitch converts at 2–5%. The relationship is worth 20× the cold pitch.

**The practical calendar**:
- Month 0 (company formed): Begin mapping VC landscape, identify 30 target investors
- Month 1–4: Start "advice meetings" with 10 investors — "not raising, want your perspective on this market"
- Month 3: Begin monthly investor updates to all contacts (whether investor or not)
- Month 5–10: Continue updates, occasional "here's what we learned" touchpoints
- Month 8: Begin formal raise process — these investors already know you, your metrics, your progress

### The "Give Before You Ask" Framework

**How to add value to VCs before pitching**:
1. **Share research**: If you discover something interesting about the physical world intelligence market, send it to your target VCs. "Thought you'd find this interesting — BeMyEye just raised and their revenue is publicly disclosed at $35M. Here's what it means for the market."
2. **Make warm intros**: If a VC is interested in a deal and you know someone in that company, make the intro. Banked goodwill.
3. **Write content they share**: A sharp LinkedIn post about "Why CPG brands are flying blind" that VCs forward to their portfolio companies positions you as a domain expert.
4. **Refer founder introductions**: If you meet a founder who's a good fit for a VC's portfolio, make the intro. VCs remember this.
5. **Show up to their events**: Most seed VCs host founder dinners, demo days, or portfolio events. Attend, be interesting, don't pitch.

## Part 2: Mapping the VC Landscape

### Tier Structure

**Tier 1 — Lead investors who will set your round terms**:
These are the 3–5 investors you most want as your lead. They write $1M+ checks. They sit on your board. Choose based on: (a) domain expertise in marketplaces/gig economy/AI, (b) portfolio that can help you (CPG relationships? other marketplace operators?), (c) founders who speak highly of them.

**Tier 2 — Strategic co-investors**:
These are 5–8 investors who will follow a lead but add value. Often angels with deep CPG/retail industry expertise. Check size: $100–500K. Value is access to their networks, not capital.

**Tier 3 — Angels**:
10–20 individual angels. CPG brand managers who've become investors. Ex-gig economy operators. Former BeMyEye executives. Check size: $25–250K. Value is social proof and operational expertise.

## Part 3: The First Meeting

### What VCs Are Actually Evaluating

Most founders think the first meeting is about the pitch. It isn't. VCs in a first meeting are evaluating:

1. **Do I believe this founder is exceptional?** — Not smart. Exceptional. The kind of person who will be relentlessly resourceful, honest when things go wrong, and inspiring to a team. 80% of the first meeting is this question.
2. **Do I believe this market is real and large?** — Not whether the founder has proven it, but whether the hypothesis is credible.
3. **Is the founder's insight non-obvious?** — If their thesis is something everyone knows, it's not interesting. VCs want to back people who see something others don't.
4. **Would I enjoy being in business with this person for 10 years?** — VCs spend more time with their founders than with their families. Chemistry matters.

**What VCs are NOT primarily evaluating in a first meeting**:
- Whether your financial model is accurate (it isn't)
- Whether you have product-market fit proven (you don't at seed)
- The specific features in your product roadmap

### How to Tell the Founding Story

The most compelling founding stories have three elements:

**1. The personal insight**: "I [saw/experienced/understood] something that nobody else was paying attention to. Here's what I noticed."
- For Mosaic: "I watched CPG brand managers spend thousands on mystery shopping for data that was weeks old. Meanwhile, millions of people were walking past those exact stores every day with smartphones in their pockets. The data existed — no one had built the infrastructure to collect it."

**2. The "why now" moment**: "This is possible now because [something changed]. This wasn't buildable 5 years ago."
- For Mosaic: "Three things converged in 2025: on-device AI models can run face redaction in real-time, so GDPR is solvable without cloud roundtrip. The gig economy created 50M+ people comfortable completing smartphone tasks for micro-pay. And enterprise CPG budgets for competitive intelligence have grown 40% in 3 years while existing solutions haven't innovated."

**3. The unfair advantage**: "Why are we the ones to build this? What do we know or have that others don't?"
- For Mosaic: "I spent 5 years at [CPG company] and personally managed a $500K competitive intelligence budget. I know exactly how VPs of Category Management buy this, what the ROI conversation looks like, and which 20 brands will sign up in the first 6 months. My co-founder shipped [X consumer app] used by [Y users] — he can build the agent experience that keeps a 22-year-old completing tasks on her morning commute."

### Questions to Ask VCs That Make Them Remember You

Most founders pitch to VCs. Great founders have conversations with VCs. Ask:

1. "You've seen dozens of marketplace companies. What's the single biggest mistake you've seen founders make in the supply-acquisition phase?" — Makes the VC share proprietary knowledge; you look sophisticated for asking.
2. "What's your current hypothesis on how GDPR plays out for crowdsourced data businesses? We have a view, but I'm curious if your portfolio has navigated this." — Shows you're thinking about hard problems; creates a dialogue.
3. "You backed [portfolio company]. What did they do differently in their first 12 months that you'd want to see us replicate?" — Shows you've done homework; flatters them without being sycophantic.
4. "Who else should we be talking to for this raise? Not competitors — other investors who might be interested, or founders who've navigated similar two-sided market dynamics?" — Shows confidence (you're not asking for their money, you're having a peer conversation).

### The "We're Not Raising Yet" First Meeting

The most powerful frame for early VC meetings: you are not pitching. You are asking for advice.

**Opening**: "We're 8 months from our formal seed raise. We're not asking for capital today — we're building relationships with investors we want to work with long-term. What would you want to see us prove in the next 8 months to make this a compelling seed investment?"

**Why this works**:
- Takes pressure off both sides — VCs can give honest feedback without worrying about deal FOMO
- You learn exactly what each VC needs to see (now you can deliver it)
- You start the clock on a relationship, not a transaction
- VCs who give specific guidance feel invested in your success — they want to see you prove them right

### Following Up After the First Meeting

**Within 24 hours**: Send a brief, specific thank-you email. Not "thanks for your time." Specific: "Loved your point about enterprise NRR compounding faster than new logo acquisition — you're right that we should front-load our pilot quality. I'm going to test the following specific change in our next pilot [X]."

**2 weeks later**: Brief update on what you tested and what you learned. Shows you execute on feedback, not just receive it.

**Monthly from there**: Monthly investor update (template below).

**What not to do**: Follow up asking "Any thoughts on timing?" or "Did you discuss us internally?" This signals desperation. Let the relationship develop at its own pace.

## Part 4: Managing the Fundraising Process

### Running a Competitive Process

A competitive fundraising process is one where multiple investors are interested simultaneously and feel social pressure to make a decision. This is manufactured through timing, not deception.

**How to create urgency legitimately**:
1. **Compress the timeline**: Start all first meetings in a 2-week window. Everyone has their first meeting roughly simultaneously.
2. **Use social proof without lying**: "We've had 8 first meetings in the past 2 weeks and the response has been strong" is honest if it's true. "We're being heavily courted by [specific firm]" is too specific unless true.
3. **Set a timeline**: "We're planning to close this round by [Month X]. We're building conviction with 3–5 investors and hope to make final decisions by then." This creates a natural deadline.
4. **Use term sheets strategically**: When the first term sheet arrives, immediately let other interested investors know: "We received a term sheet we're evaluating. Want to share our current round dynamics if you're interested in moving forward." This creates FOMO without being dishonest.

### How to Use Term Sheets to Create FOMO

**When you receive Term Sheet A**:
- Call your 3 other most interested investors personally (don't email): "I wanted to call before sending a formal update — we just received a term sheet. We want to honor the process with [Fund A], but we're also very interested in finding the right lead. Do you have enough conviction to move quickly?"
- This gives each investor the chance to step up. Some won't. The ones who do are your leads.

**What to say when asked about terms**:
- You can share: valuation cap, structure (SAFE or priced round), round size
- You should not share: specific investor names without their consent

### After a "No" — Relationship Maintenance

The VC who passes today may lead your Series A. Treat every "no" as a deferred "yes."

**After a pass**:
- Thank them specifically for their feedback ("Your insight about agent retention being the critical early metric is exactly right — we're building our Week 1 onboarding around this")
- Ask: "Would you be open to staying on our investor update list? We'll send monthly updates — would love your perspective as we progress."
- 80% will say yes. Now you have a relationship with a VC who passed. In 6 months, they'll see your metrics and sometimes lead the next round.

**The pass-to-investment conversion**: Studies of successful fundraising suggest 20–30% of investors who pass at pre-seed invest at seed or Series A when founders stayed in touch consistently with compelling updates.

## Part 6: Content Strategy for Inbound VC Interest

### Building a Thought Leadership Presence

The goal: VCs come to *you* because they've been following your thinking on physical world intelligence.

**Platform**: LinkedIn (for B2B/enterprise VC audience) and Substack (for longer-form analysis).

**Content cadence**: 2× LinkedIn posts per week. 1× Substack per month.

**Content themes that attract VC attention**:
1. **Data stories**: "We found that 23% of oat milk SKUs were out of stock in London Tesco stores on a Monday morning. Here's what that means for CPG brands." (Real data from real tasks = credibility + virality)
2. **Market insight**: "BeMyEye just reported $35M in revenue with enterprise-only access. Here's why there's a 10× larger market they're ignoring."
3. **Building in public**: "Week 1 of running an AI-native startup with 2 people. Here's what we built, what the AI tools got wrong, and our actual burn rate."
4. **Counter-narrative**: "Why GDPR is Mosaic's moat, not its obstacle." (Contrarian, smart, specific to our business)

**The compounding effect**: A LinkedIn post that a VC shares to their network brings warm inbound from investors who read that VC's feed. Your $0 content budget does the work of a $50K PR campaign.

### Cold Outreach When Warm Intros Aren't Available

Sometimes you don't have a warm intro. Cold outreach works when it's targeted, brief, and adds value immediately.

**The effective cold email formula**:

```
Subject: [Specific company from their portfolio] → [your company] connection

Hi [Name],

You backed [portfolio company]. I'm building Mosaic — same category in a different vertical (crowdsourced visual intelligence for physical world data, not [portfolio company's space]).

One thing I've learned that might be useful for [portfolio company]: [specific insight about their business, not yours].

I'm raising a $500K pre-seed in Q2. Would value 20 minutes of your perspective on whether this is a compelling market.

[Your name]
```

**What makes this work**:
- Reference their portfolio specifically → shows you did homework
- Open with something useful to them, not a pitch
- One-sentence company description only
- Specific ask (20 minutes) with specific timing (Q2)
- 6 sentences total

**Expected conversion**: 15–20% response rate from cold emails that follow this format vs 3–5% for generic cold pitches.

- `Warm` — 3+ meetings, active interest
- `Hot` — in diligence or term sheet discussion
