## The Problem With Our Current Numbers

The current deck projects 15,000 active agents by Month 18 and implies a path to massive scale that VCs will immediately pattern-match against real marketplace history. The moment a Sequoia or Andreessen partner asks "what's your Year 5 active collector count?" and we say "38 million," the meeting is over.

Here's the reality check:

**Uber**: Founded 2009. After 15 years and $25B+ raised, ~10M active drivers globally.
**DoorDash**: Founded 2013. After a decade, ~7M "Dashers" (many part-time/inactive). Active monthly drivers estimated at 1–2M.
**TaskRabbit**: Founded 2008. After 16 years, ~700,000 registered Taskers globally. Active monthly: ~150,000–200,000.
**Fiverr**: Founded 2010. ~4M registered sellers after 14 years. Active monthly sellers: ~800,000.
**Mechanical Turk**: Amazon's crowdsource platform since 2005. Estimated 500,000–600,000 workers, ~100,000 active monthly. This is after nearly 20 years with Amazon's full infrastructure.
**Field Agent**: Founded 2010. ~3M registered agents, enterprise-only, after 14 years. Estimated 500,000+ having completed at least one task.
**BeMyEye**: Founded 2012. ~3M "contributors" cited in marketing. Likely 200,000–400,000 active in any given month. $35M revenue after 13 years.

**The pattern**: The largest gig platforms in the world took 10–15 years and billions in capital to reach 1–10M *active* workers. "Registered users" is a vanity metric — active collectors who complete tasks regularly are the real number, and it's always 10–20% of registered.

## Revised Collector Growth — Credible Projections

### Definitional Framework

Before any projection, we need definitional precision that VCs respect:

- **Registered collectors**: Has downloaded app and completed onboarding. Vanity metric.
- **Activated collectors**: Has completed at least one paid task. Meaningful.
- **Monthly active collectors (MAC)**: Completes at least one task in the calendar month. The operating number.
- **Power collectors**: Completes 20+ tasks/month. Top 20% who produce 70–80% of tasks.

All projections below use **monthly active collectors (MAC)** as the primary metric.

This is the number that justifies "category leader" positioning. Not 38M, but 1M monthly active data collectors producing structured AI output is a genuinely extraordinary asset.

### Why These Numbers Are More Defensible

**1. They pass the comparables test.** Every VC worth talking to knows the Field Agent numbers, has seen the BeMyEye deck, and has portfolio companies that have tried to build gig marketplaces. 3–5K MAC at Year 1 is a number they've seen before. 38M is a number that ends the conversation.

**2. They're supply-constrained by geography.** Unlike DoorDash (every restaurant in every city is a potential task site), Mosaic tasks are posted by enterprise customers who need specific locations covered. Supply grows with demand. You can't have 500K collectors with only 50 enterprise customers posting tasks.

**3. Retention math is honest.** Gig platforms consistently see 60–70% annual churn in their active worker base. Building in realistic churn means the registered base is always 5–8× the active base. This is what investors have seen in their portfolio data.

**4. The commute-mode retention thesis is differentiated but unproven.** Our hypothesis is that agents who earn $14–20 per commute (vs $8–12 per hour on DoorDash) will churn far less. That's a credible hypothesis. But in the deck, we should present it as a hypothesis with a pilot validation plan, not a proven fact.

## Revised Financial Projections

At full enterprise pricing, the math is driven by MAC and average tasks per active collector per month.

**Task volume assumptions:**
- Average power collector: 40 tasks/month
- Average regular MAC: 12 tasks/month
- Power collectors: 20% of MAC, produce 65% of tasks
- Regular MACs: 80% of MAC, produce 35% of tasks

*Note: Task volume is also constrained by enterprise demand — these numbers assume enough customer-side demand to fill collector capacity.*

### Revised Three-Tier Revenue Model

The existing revenue model is reasonable on the customer side — the issue isn't task pricing, it's the implied collector volumes. Here's the revised model grounded in the MAC projections above.

#### Year 1 (Conservative)
- Enterprise customers: 15–25 (founder-led sales, tight CPG beachhead)
- SMB subscribers: 150–300
- Consumer active users: 1,500–3,000
- Tasks/month at Year 1 end: 40,000–70,000
- **ARR at Year 1 end: $2.0M–$3.5M**

The original model's $3M–$4.25M ARR at Month 12 is possible but assumes the high end of enterprise customer acquisition pace. Revise to $2M–$3.5M as the target range with $3M as the stretch case.

#### Year 2 (Post-Seed)
- Enterprise customers: 40–60
- SMB subscribers: 500–800
- Consumer active users: 8,000–15,000
- Tasks/month at Year 2 end: 150,000–250,000
- **ARR at Year 2 end: $6M–$9M**

This is the Series A trigger range. Still defensible. 50 enterprise customers at $5,500/month average = $3.3M ARR from enterprise alone. Add SMB and consumer and you're at $6–9M.

#### Year 3
- Enterprise customers: 100–150
- SMB subscribers: 1,500–2,500
- Consumer active users: 40,000–80,000
- Tasks/month: 400,000–700,000
- Training data revenue begins to compound
- **ARR at Year 3: $18M–$28M**

#### Year 5
- Enterprise customers: 300–500 (multi-geography, multi-use-case)
- SMB subscribers: 8,000–15,000
- Consumer active users: 300,000–600,000
- Tasks/month: 2M–4M
- Training data licensing: $3M–$8M/month at this scale
- **ARR at Year 5: $80M–$150M**

#### Year 10
- The platform with 800K–1.5M MAC processing 10M+ tasks/month
- Enterprise is the revenue core; consumer is the density driver
- Training data is a $20M+/month revenue stream at 95% margin
- **ARR at Year 10: $300M–$600M**

This is the honest 10-year vision. Not $38M active collectors and implied trillion-dollar revenue. A $300–600M ARR business is an exceptional outcome — it justifies a $3B–$6B exit or public listing, which is in the top 1% of venture outcomes.

## Revised Market Size Claims

### Current Deck: TAM $35B / SAM $8.5B / SOM $350M (Year 3)

The TAM and SAM are defensible. The SOM needs revision.

**TAM: $35B** — Keep this. Physical world intelligence market is real and well-documented. IRI, Nielsen, mystery shopping, insurance field services all validate it. Cite: Mordor Intelligence (Retail Intelligence Market, $38.5B by 2028), Verified Market Research, GlobalData.

**SAM: $8.5B** — Revise slightly. The addressable segment for a crowdsourced, AI-native, on-demand platform is plausibly $5–8B. The $8.5B number requires the consumer market to be large. Keep $7–8B but be explicit about the assumptions.

**SOM (Year 3): Revise from $350M to $75–120M.**

The original SOM implies "50K enterprise customers ($66K ACV avg) + 2M SMB customers + 50M consumer credits" in Year 3. This is wildly disconnected from the collector base reality. 50K enterprise customers requires a sales team of 500+ people and $200M+ in sales infrastructure. 2M SMB customers requires 100K+ new customers per month.

**Revised SOM (Year 3):**
- 120 enterprise customers × $66K ACV = $7.9M ARR
- 2,000 SMB customers × $3K ACV = $6M ARR
- 70,000 consumer users × $120 annual spend = $8.4M ARR
- Training data: $3M ARR
- **Total Year 3 SOM: ~$25M ARR ($25M ARR captured from a $7B SAM = 0.36% market share)**

This is credible. 0.36% market share in Year 3 is exactly where a well-funded marketplace startup should be. And it still grows to the $300–600M range by Year 10, which is the VC-scale outcome.

## Why $20M Seed Valuation Is Still Justified

The most common VC objection to revising down the big numbers is: "if you're projecting less, why should we pay the same valuation?" Here's the argument.

**The valuation is justified by the defensibility of the architecture, not the size of the Year 10 projection.**

1. **The training data moat compounds regardless of scale.** 800K active collectors producing 10M tasks/month generates ~80M images/month with geolocation, timestamp, and structured AI extraction. That dataset — after 5 years — is worth more than the platform revenue. No one can buy their way into that dataset.

2. **The supply-side network effect is still real.** A platform with 50,000 verified, trained, active collectors in 40 cities has a moat that would take a competitor 3–5 years and $50M to replicate. This defensibility exists whether we have 50K or 5M collectors.

3. **The CPG beachhead is extremely high-value territory.** The 50 largest CPG companies globally spend $35B/year on field intelligence. Getting 10% of 50 of those companies as customers is $350M in TAM concentration — in the hands of a two-person team. That concentration justifies premium entry pricing.

4. **The $20M pre-money is a market-rate seed for this category.** Enterprise data marketplace with defensible AI pipeline, pre-seed with strong founding team and clear CPG beachhead: $15–25M pre-money is standard in 2026. Comparable: Premise Data raised at $20M pre-money in 2015. Zenchef raised at $25M. The valuation is comps-justified.

5. **Realistic numbers signal maturity.** A VC who sees us revise from "38M collectors" to "1.5M collectors" doesn't think we're less ambitious — they think we've done the work. Intellectual honesty about growth curves is a trust signal. Inflated projections are a trust-destruction signal.

**The ask**: $5M at $20M pre-money. That's 20% dilution. It buys 18 months of runway to prove two cities, three tiers, and $3.5M ARR. At $3.5M ARR growing 3× YoY, the Series A conversation begins at $35–50M pre-money. That's a 2–2.5× step-up in 18 months on the pre-money — exactly what early-stage VCs model.
