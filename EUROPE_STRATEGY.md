## The European Opportunity in One Paragraph

The US crowdsourced visual intelligence market has Field Agent (strong, US-centric), Premise Data (global but survey-focused), and Trax (hardware, enterprise). In Europe, the equivalent is BeMyEye — a London-founded company with 3M+ contributors across 21 countries that serves Nestlé, PepsiCo, and L'Oréal. But BeMyEye has $35M in revenue, 79 employees, and no consumer/SMB tier. It's the European version of the same enterprise-only gap that Mosaic is built to exploit in the US. European CPG brand HQs (Unilever in London/Rotterdam, Nestlé in Vevey, Danone in Paris, L'Oréal in Paris, Reckitt in Slough, Heineken in Amsterdam) are already spending on retail intelligence. Mosaic can walk into their procurement meetings with a product that beats BeMyEye on AI quality, speed, self-serve access, and consumer tier — and pitch it in English to people who read English-language research.

**The strategic case for Europe first**: US players haven't scaled here. BeMyEye is beatable. EU enterprise sales cycles reward trust-building, meaning early relationships compound. And EU validation — especially from recognizable names like Unilever or Danone — carries enormous credibility in US fundraising conversations.

## Part 1: Five European Cities to Launch First

### City Selection Methodology

Each city scored on five factors:
1. **Gig worker density** — existing platform worker base to recruit from
2. **CPG brand proximity** — HQ or major regional office presence
3. **English proficiency** — for early B2B sales without localization overhead
4. **Regulatory friendliness** — toward gig platforms and data businesses
5. **Competitive gap** — BeMyEye presence vs available market

**Why first**: Unilever UK HQ (Blackfriars), Reckitt HQ (Slough, 20 min), numerous CPG regional offices. London has the UK's 1.7M gig worker base, with over 21% of all UK gig economy activity concentrated here. English is native. This exact pool — urban, phone-equipped, comfortable with gig task apps — is Mosaic's primary recruitment audience. Facebook Groups for "UK gig workers" and Courier-focused communities are active and recruitable.

**Regulatory note**: ICO (UK) is generally more pragmatic than some EU DPAs on legitimate interests grounds. UK GDPR has same on-device redaction implications as EU GDPR.

**Agent acquisition target**: 500 verified agents within 6 weeks, London only.

### City 2: Amsterdam (Launch Month 1, simultaneous with London)

**Why second**: Unilever Foods & Refreshment maintains offices in Rotterdam (30 min), and Amsterdam is the operating HQ of Heineken, Philips, and multiple FMCG divisions. The Netherlands' court ruled against Deliveroo's employment model (2021) — this means task-based independent work must be structured carefully (see GDPR section below for agent data compliance).

**CPG targets**: Heineken (Amsterdam), Unilever Netherlands, FrieslandCampina (Amersfoort, 40 km), Royal Wessanen.

**Gig worker note**: Germany's gig economy is growing but faces stronger employee-reclassification pressure. **CPG targets**: Beiersdorf (Hamburg, reachable), Henkel (Düsseldorf, 5-hour train), Dr. Oetker (Bielefeld), Miele (enterprise home appliance intelligence).

## Part 2: EU Regulatory Landscape — GDPR Deep Dive

### The Core GDPR Question for Mosaic

Mosaic's agents take photos in public and semi-public spaces (retail stores, streets, parks) using their smartphones. The photos may incidentally capture identifiable people — shoppers, staff, passersby. The on-device face redaction runs before any image leaves the device. This is the key fact that changes the entire GDPR analysis.

**The principle**: If faces are redacted before upload, the uploaded images no longer contain personal data relating to identifiable individuals. They become anonymized data — and GDPR doesn't apply to anonymized data at all (Recital 26).

**The caveat**: The redaction must be genuinely irreversible. If Mosaic retains the unredacted version anywhere, it retains personal data and all GDPR obligations apply to that version. Mosaic's architecture must be: redact on device → upload only redacted image → unredacted image is never transmitted or stored.

---

### GDPR Lawful Basis for Each Data Category

**1. Agent data (name, email, location, payment)**
- Lawful basis: **Contract (Article 6(1)(b))** — processing is necessary to perform the service agreement with the agent.
- Retention: During active relationship + 7 years for tax/payment records (legal obligation basis).
- Special consideration: Location data is sensitive. Mosaic should process only task-time location (not continuous tracking), disclose this clearly in the agent app, and store only the location data necessary for task verification.

**2. Customer data (company name, contacts, billing)**
- Lawful basis: **Contract (Article 6(1)(b))** for service delivery; **Legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f))** for marketing communications to existing customers.
- No special category data involved.

**3. Task images after on-device redaction**
- Post-redaction: **Not personal data** (Recital 26, anonymized data). GDPR does not apply.
- Pre-redaction, on device only: **Legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f))** — the data subject's privacy interest is protected because the data never leaves the device unredacted. The Legitimate Interests Assessment (LIA) must document this balance explicitly.

**4. Task location data (the GPS coordinates of task completion)**
- This is not personal data on its own unless it reveals the agent's home address or daily patterns. Point-in-time task completion coordinates are low risk.
- Mosaic should avoid building detailed movement profiles of agents — that would cross into special category territory (location data enabling inference of home, place of worship, medical facilities visited, etc.).

**5. AI training data (labeled images)**
- Since faces are redacted, the images don't contain personal data. Selling anonymized retail/public space images to AI labs is not a GDPR activity.
- **Important**: Mosaic must ensure the redaction process doesn't have a re-identification risk. Any metadata (EXIF data, store identifiers, precise GPS) could in theory be combined with third-party data to re-identify subjects. Strip or hash EXIF before export.

---

### Is On-Device Redaction Sufficient for GDPR Compliance?

**Short answer**: Yes for images. No for agent data processing.

On-device redaction is **the key technical safeguard** that makes Mosaic's image capture model GDPR-defensible. When no identifiable person appears in the uploaded image, there is no personal data in the image, and GDPR doesn't apply to that image.

However, GDPR compliance requires more than just image redaction:

**Required additional measures**:
1. **Privacy Policy** (Articles 13/14): Agents and customers must receive clear notice of what data is collected, why, how long it's retained, and their rights.
2. **DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment)** (Article 35): Required when processing is "likely to result in a high risk." Systematic monitoring of public spaces is explicitly listed as a high-risk activity in GDPR Article 35(3)(c). Mosaic must complete a DPIA before launch in each EU country.
3. **Record of Processing Activities (ROPA)** (Article 30): Required for any organization processing personal data as part of commercial activity. Must list all processing activities, purposes, lawful bases, data categories, retention periods, and security measures.
4. **Data Processing Agreements (DPAs)**: With every cloud provider (AWS, Google), payment processor (Stripe), and any third party that processes personal data on Mosaic's behalf.
5. **EU Representative** (Article 27): If Mosaic is incorporated outside the EU, it must appoint an EU-based representative for data protection matters. (Solved by incorporating an EU entity or using a service like DP-Rep.)
6. **Privacy-by-design** (Article 25): On-device redaction is the primary PbD measure. Additional measures: minimal data collection, encrypted storage, agent location data deleted after 30 days, no behavioral profiling of agents beyond task completion metrics.

**The DPIA for Mosaic's core use case** must address:
- Systematic observation of publicly accessible areas (task locations)
- Processing of location data (agent GPS)
- Processing of images that could contain personal data before redaction
- The safeguard (on-device redaction) and why it reduces risk to acceptable levels

### GDPR vs CCPA vs BIPA: Comparative Difficulty

| Dimension | GDPR (EU) | CCPA (California) | BIPA (Illinois) |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Applies to** | Anyone processing EU residents' data | California consumers | Anyone collecting biometric data in IL |
| **Key risk for Mosaic** | Agent/customer data + incidental face capture | Consumer data rights | Face data if captured (even briefly) |
| **On-device redaction solves?** | Yes for images, no for agent data | Largely yes | Yes — if no face data ever processed |
| **Penalties** | €20M or 4% global revenue (higher) | $7,500/intentional violation | $1,000–5,000 per person per violation (class action risk) |
| **Class action risk** | Low (European legal system, no class action culture) | Moderate | VERY HIGH — plaintiff attorneys actively file BIPA class actions |
| **Enforcement culture** | Bureaucratic, formal, DPA-driven | Growing (AG enforcement) | Litigation-driven, unpredictable |
| **Practical hardship** | High paperwork, DPIAs, ROPA | Medium | Highest legal risk per dollar of activity |
| **Verdict** | More process, less litigation risk | Manageable | Most dangerous — avoid Illinois until legal opinion |

**Conclusion**: GDPR is more paperwork than CCPA, but BIPA is the most dangerous by far because of class action litigation. On-device redaction eliminates BIPA risk entirely (no biometric data processed), partially reduces GDPR risk (no image personal data), and has minimal impact on CCPA (non-biometric data rights still apply).

**Europe is not harder than Illinois. It's harder than California but safer in litigation terms.**

## Part 4: European Go-to-Market Differences vs US

### Enterprise Sales Culture

**EU vs US procurement timelines**: EU enterprise sales cycles are on average 20–30% longer than US equivalents. A US enterprise deal that closes in 30 days will take 45–60 days in the UK and 90+ in France or Germany. Plan for this in financial projections.

**Trust signals that matter in Europe**:
- GDPR compliance documentation (privacy policy, DPA, LIA) — European procurement teams will ask for this. US companies that don't have it are immediately disqualified.
- SOC 2 equivalent: ISO 27001 or ISO 27001:2022 is more commonly required in EU than SOC 2 (US standard). Plan to pursue ISO 27001 rather than SOC 2 if Europe is the priority.
- Local entity: Having a UK Ltd or BV (Dutch entity) builds trust. "Incorporated in Delaware" is a red flag for EU procurement.
- GDPR Data Processing Agreement (DPA) template: Have this ready on Day 1. EU customers will not sign without it.

**Language**: Sell in English in London, Amsterdam, Berlin (management level). Barcelona requires Spanish for agent recruitment and some sales. Paris genuinely requires French for anything below VP level.

**Payment methods**: EU businesses expect SEPA bank transfer invoicing, not credit card. Enterprise procurement in Europe uses invoice + bank transfer. Consumer/SMB tier can use card (Stripe handles EUR). For enterprise: offer SEPA Direct Debit and bank transfer.

**VAT**: Mosaic must register for VAT in each EU country where it has customers above the relevant threshold. UK has separate VAT registration. This is operational overhead — factor it in.

### The BeMyEye Competitive Advantage

BeMyEye has established relationships with every major European CPG brand. This means:
- Buyers know the category exists and have budget
- The conversation starts at "why switch" not "what is this"
- Mosaic's sales narrative: "BeMyEye gives you field data. We give you field data + AI extraction + consumer tier + modern API + 24-hour turnaround vs their 48–72 hours"
- BeMyEye's enterprise-only model leaves a massive self-serve gap — brand managers at smaller European brands (€50M–€500M) have no product to use

## Part 5: "EU First" Strategic Argument

### Could Mosaic Launch in Europe BEFORE the US?

**The case for EU-first**:
1. **Weaker competition**: BeMyEye is beatable. Field Agent barely exists in Europe. Premise Data has thin EU presence. No "Uber for gig tasks" competitor has built a consumer tier in Europe.
2. **First-mover moat**: Enterprise relationships take 2–3 years to fully compound. Getting into Unilever or Danone before any competitor creates a moat that's extremely hard to displace.
3. **Fundraising narrative**: "We launched in Europe first, signed Unilever and Danone as customers, and now we're bringing this to the US where the market is even larger" is a compelling Series A story for US investors who love European validation.
4. **GDPR as moat**: Building GDPR compliance properly from Day 1 is expensive, but it becomes a barrier to entry. Late-entering US competitors have to retrofit GDPR — Mosaic has it baked in.
5. **Lower agent acquisition cost**: EU gig workers earn less per task from existing platforms — Mosaic's $2–10/task is competitive. In San Francisco, the same pay rate feels low.

**The case against EU-first**:
1. **Enterprise sales are harder**: EU procurement is slower. It will take longer to close first logos.
2. **No home court**: US founders building for US market is the default VC assumption. EU launch requires explaining "why not US first."
3. **Regulatory overhead**: GDPR paperwork, VAT registration, local entity costs real money and founder time.
4. **Agent supply**: London has great supply, but Amsterdam/Berlin require localized recruitment.

**Verdict**: EU is not "instead of US" — it's "in parallel or shortly after." The ideal sequence is: **Atlanta/Dallas US launch (Month 1) + London/Amsterdam EU (Month 2–3)**. This gives you US traction to show US investors AND European blue-chip customers to show "international" credibility. If forced to choose one geography at pre-seed: choose US for faster revenue (US enterprise closes faster) and use EU as a Series A expansion story.

### The "EU Validation → US Fundraise" Playbook

Month 1–6: Launch US (Atlanta, Dallas)
Month 3: London soft launch — 1 paid pilot with a UK CPG brand
Month 6: Amsterdam launch, expand London
Month 9–12: First EU enterprise contracts signed (Reckitt, Unilever, or Heineken)
Month 12–18: US Series A pitch — "We have $X ARR in the US AND European enterprise contracts with Unilever and Heineken. We're building the global physical world intelligence layer, not just a US retail tool."

This arc is significantly more compelling for a $10M+ Series A than a US-only story.

## Part 6: European Pricing Strategy

### Does €-Pricing Need to Differ from $-Pricing?

**EUR vs USD parity**: As of March 2026, €1 ≈ $1.08. The pricing differential is minimal. However, pricing psychology in Europe differs:

**Recommendations**:
1. **Set prices in EUR for EU customers** — invoicing in USD creates FX friction for European buyers and signals "US company that didn't bother."
2. **Price at parity or slightly below USD** — a €49/month Spark tier vs $49/month is psychologically equivalent. Don't use the FX rate to charge €53 — it reads as opportunistic.
3. **Agent payouts in local currency**: UK agents paid in GBP, EU agents in EUR. Wise (TransferWise) or Stripe payouts handle this efficiently.
4. **Enterprise contracts in EUR**: Unilever/Heineken procurement will require EUR contracts with VAT.
5. **SMB self-serve**: Stripe handles multicurrency — EUR pricing is a config change, not a rebuild.

**Tactical note**: L'Oréal and Danone procurement teams will expect to negotiate on volume. Have a EUR price book with 10–15% headroom for negotiation without destroying margin.
